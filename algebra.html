1. Angles and Lines

Understanding the relationships between angles and lines is the foundation of geometry. This includes pairs of angles and the specific angles formed when a line intersects parallel lines.

Angle Relationships

Angle Types
Complementary Angles: Two angles that sum to \(90^\circ\).
Supplementary Angles: Two angles that sum to \(180^\circ\).
Step 1: Let the angle be \(x\). Its complement is \(90 - x\).
Step 2: Set up the equation: \( x = (90 - x) + 24 \).
Step 3: Solve for \(x\): \( x = 114 - x \implies 2x = 114 \implies x = 57^\circ \).
The angle is \(57^\circ\).

Example 2: Two supplementary angles are in the ratio 4:5. Find the measure of the smaller angle.

Step 1: Let the angles be \(4x\) and \(5x\).
Step 2: Since they are supplementary, their sum is \(180^\circ\): \(4x + 5x = 180^\circ\).
Step 3: Solve for x: \(9x = 180^\circ \implies x = 20^\circ\).
Step 4: The smaller angle is \(4x\): \(4(20^\circ) = 80^\circ\).
The smaller angle is \(80^\circ\).

Parallel Lines and Transversals

When a transversal line intersects two parallel lines, specific pairs of angles are created that are either equal or supplementary.

If lines are parallel:
Alternate Interior Angles are equal.
Corresponding Angles are equal.
Alternate Exterior Angles are equal.
Consecutive Interior Angles are supplementary (sum to \(180^\circ\)).

Example 3: Two parallel lines are cut by a transversal. If a corresponding angle is \( (3x - 10)^\circ \) and its pair is \( (2x + 15)^\circ \), find the value of x.

Step 1: Corresponding angles formed by parallel lines are equal.
Step 2: Set the expressions equal: \( 3x - 10 = 2x + 15 \).
Step 3: Solve for x: \( x = 25 \).
The value of x is 25.

Example 4: Two parallel lines are cut by a transversal. Two consecutive interior angles are \( (x+30)^\circ \) and \( (2x)^\circ \). Find the measure of both angles.

Step 1: Consecutive interior angles are supplementary.
Step 2: Set their sum equal to 180: \( (x+30) + (2x) = 180 \).
Step 3: Solve for x: \( 3x + 30 = 180 \implies 3x = 150 \implies x = 50 \).
Step 4: Find the measure of each angle:
First angle: \( 50 + 30 = 80^\circ \)
Second angle: \( 2(50) = 100^\circ \)
The angles are \(80^\circ\) and \(100^\circ\).

Example 5: In the diagram below, line L is parallel to line M. What is the value of x?

Step 1: The angle marked \(110^\circ\) (top-left) and the angle marked \(x\) (bottom-left) are corresponding angles.
Step 2: When parallel lines are cut by a transversal, corresponding angles are equal.
Step 3: Therefore, we can set the values equal to each other.
The value of x is \(110^\circ\).

Example 6: Two parallel lines L and M are cut by a transversal. Three consecutive angles are marked, and one is 120Â°, as shown in the diagram. Find the values of x and y.

Step 1: The angle marked 120Â° and angle y are alternate exterior angles. When parallel lines are cut by a transversal, alternate exterior angles are equal. Therefore, \(y = 120^\circ\).
Step 2: Angles x and y form a linear pair on line M, meaning they are supplementary (sum to 180Â°).
Step 3: Since \(y = 120^\circ\), we can find x using the supplementary angle relationship: \[ x + 120^\circ = 180^\circ \] \[ x = 60^\circ \]
The values are \(x = 60^\circ\) and \(y = 120^\circ\).

2. Triangles

Triangles are fundamental three-sided polygons. Their properties, including side-angle relationships and area calculations, are frequently tested.

Properties and Theorems

Key Triangle Properties:
The sum of the interior angles is always \(180^\circ\).
The measure of an exterior angle equals the sum of the two opposite interior angles.
The length of any side of a triangle is less than the sum of the other two sides (Triangle Inequality Theorem).
Area = \(\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}\)

Example 1: The angles of a triangle are in the ratio 2:3:5. What is the measure of the largest angle?

Step 1: Let the angles be \(2x, 3x, 5x\). Their sum is \(180^\circ\).
Step 2: \( 2x + 3x + 5x = 180^\circ \implies 10x = 180^\circ \implies x = 18^\circ \).
Step 3: The largest angle is \(5x = 5(18^\circ) = 90^\circ \).
The largest angle is \(90^\circ\).

Example 2: Can a triangle have sides of length 4, 7, and 12?

Step 1: Check the Triangle Inequality Theorem. The sum of any two sides must be greater than the third side.
Step 2: Check all three conditions:
\(4+7 > 12 \implies 11 > 12\). This is FALSE.
Step 3: Since one condition fails, a triangle cannot be formed with these side lengths.
No, a triangle cannot be formed.

Triangle Classification

Isosceles Triangle: An isosceles triangle has two equal sides and two equal base angles (the angles opposite the equal sides).
Equilateral Triangle: An equilateral triangle has three equal sides and three equal angles, each measuring \(60^\circ\).

Example 3: An isosceles triangle has a vertex angle of \(40^\circ\). What are the measures of the base angles?

Step 1: Let the two equal base angles be \(x\).
Step 2: The sum of angles in a triangle is \(180^\circ\). \[ 40^\circ + x + x = 180^\circ \]
Step 3: Solve for x.
\( 2x = 140^\circ \)
\( x = 70^\circ \)
Each base angle measures \(70^\circ\).

Example 4: In an isosceles triangle, one of the base angles is \(75^\circ\). What is the measure of the vertex angle?

Step 1: In an isosceles triangle, the two base angles are equal. Therefore, the other base angle is also \(75^\circ\).
Step 2: Let the vertex angle be \(y\). The sum of angles in a triangle is \(180^\circ\). \[ 75^\circ + 75^\circ + y = 180^\circ \]
Step 3: Simplify and solve for y.
\( 150^\circ + y = 180^\circ \)
\( y = 180^\circ - 150^\circ = 30^\circ \)
The measure of the vertex angle is \(30^\circ\).

Example 5: An equilateral triangle has a perimeter of 24 cm. What is the length of one side?

Step 1: An equilateral triangle has three sides of equal length. Let the side length be \(s\).
Step 2: The perimeter is the sum of the side lengths: \( P = s + s + s = 3s \).
Step 3: We are given that the perimeter is 24 cm. Set up the equation: \[ 3s = 24 \]
Step 4: Solve for s by dividing both sides by 3. \[ s = \frac{24}{3} = 8 \text{ cm} \]
The length of one side is 8 cm.

Right Triangles

Right triangles have one \(90^\circ\) angle and are governed by the Pythagorean theorem. Special right triangles (45-45-90 and 30-60-90) have fixed side ratios that allow for quick calculations.

Pythagorean Theorem: \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\), where c is the hypotenuse.
45-45-90 Triangle: Sides are in the ratio \(x : x : x\sqrt{2}\).
30-60-90 Triangle: Sides are in the ratio \(x : x\sqrt{3} : 2x\).

Example 6: The hypotenuse of a 30-60-90 triangle is 12. What is the length of the longer leg?

Step 1: The side ratios are \(x : x\sqrt{3} : 2x\) (short leg : long leg : hypotenuse).
Step 2: The hypotenuse is \(2x\). We are given that it is 12. \( 2x = 12 \implies x = 6 \).
Step 3: The length of the longer leg is \(x\sqrt{3} = 6\sqrt{3}\).
The length of the longer leg is \(6\sqrt{3}\).

Example 7: A square has a diagonal of length 10. What is the area of the square?

Step 1: The diagonal of a square divides it into two 45-45-90 right triangles. The diagonal is the hypotenuse.
Step 2: The hypotenuse is \(x\sqrt{2}\). So, \( x\sqrt{2} = 10 \).
Step 3: Solve for x, the side length: \( x = \frac{10}{\sqrt{2}} = 5\sqrt{2} \).
Step 4: Area = side squared = \( (5\sqrt{2})^2 = 25 \cdot 2 = 50 \).
The area of the square is 50.

Example 8: The two legs of a right triangle are 8 cm and 15 cm. What is the length of the hypotenuse?

Step 1: Use the Pythagorean theorem: \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\), where a and b are the legs and c is the hypotenuse.
Step 2: Substitute the given values: \(8^2 + 15^2 = c^2\).
Step 3: Calculate the squares: \(64 + 225 = c^2\).
Step 4: Simplify and solve for c: \(c^2 = 289 \implies c = \sqrt{289} = 17\).
The length of the hypotenuse is 17 cm. (This is a common Pythagorean triple: 8-15-17).

Example 9: One leg of a 45-45-90 triangle is 7 units long. What is the length of the hypotenuse?

Step 1: In a 45-45-90 triangle, the two legs are equal, and the hypotenuse is \(\sqrt{2}\) times the length of a leg. The side ratios are \(x : x : x\sqrt{2}\).
Step 2: We are given that a leg (x) is 7.
Step 3: The hypotenuse is \(x\sqrt{2}\). Substitute the value of x: \(7\sqrt{2}\).
The length of the hypotenuse is \(7\sqrt{2}\) units.

Example 10: In a right triangle, the hypotenuse is 13 cm and one leg is 5 cm. What is the length of the other leg?

Step 1: Use the Pythagorean theorem: \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\). Let the known leg be \(a=5\), the hypotenuse be \(c=13\), and the unknown leg be \(b\).
Step 2: Substitute the values into the theorem: \(5^2 + b^2 = 13^2\).
Step 3: Calculate the squares: \(25 + b^2 = 169\).
Step 4: Solve for \(b^2\): \(b^2 = 169 - 25 = 144\).
Step 5: Take the square root to find b: \(b = \sqrt{144} = 12\).
The length of the other leg is 12 cm. (This is a common Pythagorean triple: 5-12-13).

Congruence and Similarity

Congruent triangles have the same size and shape (all corresponding sides and angles are equal). Similar triangles have the same shape but possibly different sizes (corresponding angles are equal, and corresponding sides are in proportion).

Congruence Postulates (Ways to prove triangles are identical):
  • SSS (Side-Side-Side): If three sides of one triangle are equal to the three corresponding sides of another triangle.
  • SAS (Side-Angle-Side): If two sides and the included angle (the angle between them) of one triangle are equal to the corresponding parts of another.
  • ASA (Angle-Side-Angle): If two angles and the included side (the side between them) of one triangle are equal to the corresponding parts of another.
  • AAS (Angle-Angle-Side): If two angles and a non-included side of one triangle are equal to the corresponding parts of another.

Similarity Postulates (Ways to prove triangles have the same shape):
  • AA (Angle-Angle): If two angles of one triangle are equal to two corresponding angles of another.
  • SSS (Side-Side-Side): If all three corresponding sides are in the same proportion.
  • SAS (Side-Angle-Side): If two corresponding sides are in proportion and their included angles are equal.

Example 11: A 6-foot tall person casts a 4-foot shadow. At the same time, a nearby flagpole casts a 20-foot shadow. How tall is the flagpole?

Step 1: The person and their shadow, and the flagpole and its shadow, form two similar right triangles by the AA similarity postulate (both have a right angle and the same angle of elevation to the sun).
Step 2: Set up a proportion of the heights to the shadow lengths. Let H be the height of the flagpole. \[ \frac{6}{4} = \frac{H}{20} \]
Step 3: Solve for H by cross-multiplying: \( 4H = 120 \implies H = 30 \) feet.
The flagpole is 30 feet tall.

Example 12 (SSS Congruence): Triangle ABC has side lengths AB = 5, BC = 7, and AC = 8. Triangle XYZ has side lengths XY = 5, YZ = 7, and XZ = 8. Are the two triangles congruent?

Step 1: Compare the corresponding sides of the two triangles.
Step 2: We are given:
\( AB = XY = 5 \)
\( BC = YZ = 7 \)
\( AC = XZ = 8 \)
Step 3: Since all three corresponding sides are equal in length, the triangles are congruent by the SSS (Side-Side-Side) postulate.
Yes, \(\triangle ABC \cong \triangle XYZ\) by SSS.

Example 13 (SAS Congruence): In \(\triangle ABC\), AB = 10 cm, \(\angle B = 50^\circ\), and BC = 15 cm. In \(\triangle DEF\), DE = 10 cm, \(\angle E = 50^\circ\), and EF = 15 cm. Are the triangles congruent?

Step 1: Identify the given information for both triangles: two sides and an angle.
Step 2: Check if the angle is the included angle (the angle between the two given sides).
In \(\triangle ABC\), \(\angle B\) is between sides AB and BC.
In \(\triangle DEF\), \(\angle E\) is between sides DE and EF.
Step 3: Compare the corresponding parts:
Side AB = Side DE = 10 cm.
Angle B = Angle E = 50Â°.
Side BC = Side EF = 15 cm.
Step 4: Since two sides and the included angle of \(\triangle ABC\) are equal to the corresponding parts of \(\triangle DEF\), the triangles are congruent by the SAS (Side-Angle-Side) postulate.
Yes, the triangles are congruent by SAS.

Example 14 (ASA Congruence): You are given that in \(\triangle GHI\), \(\angle G = 40^\circ\), side GH = 8, and \(\angle H = 60^\circ\). In \(\triangle JKL\), \(\angle J = 40^\circ\), side JK = 8, and \(\angle K = 60^\circ\). Are the triangles congruent?

Step 1: Identify the given information: two angles and a side for each triangle.
Step 2: Check if the side is the included side (the side between the two given angles).
In \(\triangle GHI\), side GH is between \(\angle G\) and \(\angle H\).
In \(\triangle JKL\), side JK is between \(\angle J\) and \(\angle K\).
Step 3: Compare the corresponding parts:
Angle G = Angle J = 40Â°.
Side GH = Side JK = 8.
Angle H = Angle K = 60Â°.
Step 4: Since two angles and the included side of \(\triangle GHI\) are equal to the corresponding parts of \(\triangle JKL\), the triangles are congruent by the ASA (Angle-Side-Angle) postulate.
Yes, the triangles are congruent by ASA.

Trigonometry in General Triangles

While SOHCAHTOA applies to right triangles, the Law of Sines and Law of Cosines can be used to solve for sides and angles in any triangle (non-right triangles included).

Law of Sines: \( \frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C} \)
(Use when you know an angle and its opposite side, plus one other piece of information).

Law of Cosines: \( c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab \cos C \)
(Use when you know two sides and the included angle (SAS), or all three sides (SSS)).

Area of a Triangle: \( \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab \sin C \)
(Use when you know two sides and the included angle).

Example 15 (Law of Sines): In \(\triangle ABC\), \( \angle A = 30^\circ \), \( \angle B = 45^\circ \), and side \(a = 10\). Find the length of side \(b\).

Step 1: We have an angle-side pair (\(\angle A\) and \(a\)), so we can use the Law of Sines.
Step 2: Set up the proportion: \( \frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} \). \[ \frac{10}{\sin 30^\circ} = \frac{b}{\sin 45^\circ} \]
Step 3: Solve for b: \( b = \frac{10 \sin 45^\circ}{\sin 30^\circ} \).
Step 4: Substitute known values (\(\sin 45^\circ = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\), \(\sin 30^\circ = \frac{1}{2}\)): \[ b = \frac{10 (\sqrt{2}/2)}{1/2} = 10\sqrt{2} \]
The length of side b is \(10\sqrt{2}\).

Example 16 (Law of Cosines): In \(\triangle PQR\), side \(p = 5\), side \(q = 7\), and the included angle \( \angle R = 60^\circ \). Find the length of side \(r\).

Step 1: We have two sides and the included angle (SAS), so we use the Law of Cosines.
Step 2: Set up the formula: \( r^2 = p^2 + q^2 - 2pq \cos R \). \[ r^2 = 5^2 + 7^2 - 2(5)(7) \cos 60^\circ \]
Step 3: Substitute \(\cos 60^\circ = \frac{1}{2}\) and calculate: \[ r^2 = 25 + 49 - 70(\frac{1}{2}) = 74 - 35 = 39 \]
Step 4: Solve for r: \( r = \sqrt{39} \).
The length of side r is \(\sqrt{39}\).

Example 17 (Area Formula): Find the area of a triangle with sides of length 8 and 10, and an included angle of \(30^\circ\).

Step 1: Use the area formula \( \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab \sin C \).
Step 2: Substitute the given values: \[ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}(8)(10) \sin 30^\circ \]
Step 3: Substitute \(\sin 30^\circ = \frac{1}{2}\) and calculate: \[ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}(80)(\frac{1}{2}) = 40(\frac{1}{2}) = 20 \]
The area of the triangle is 20 square units.

Example 18 (Law of Cosines - SSS): In \(\triangle ABC\), side a=7, side b=8, and side c=13. Find the measure of angle C.

Step 1: We have all three sides (SSS), so we use the Law of Cosines to find an angle. The formula is \( c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab \cos C \).
Step 2: Substitute the given values: \( 13^2 = 7^2 + 8^2 - 2(7)(8) \cos C \).
Step 3: Calculate the squares: \( 169 = 49 + 64 - 112 \cos C \).
Step 4: Simplify and solve for \(\cos C\): \( 169 = 113 - 112 \cos C \implies 56 = -112 \cos C \implies \cos C = -\frac{56}{112} = -\frac{1}{2} \).
Step 5: Find the angle C. The angle in a triangle whose cosine is -1/2 is \(120^\circ\).
The measure of angle C is \(120^\circ\).

Example 19 (Law of Sines): Using the information in the diagram below, find the measure of angle Y to the nearest degree.

Step 1: We have an angle-side pair (X and x), so we use the Law of Sines.
Step 2: Set up the proportion: \( \frac{\sin X}{x} = \frac{\sin Y}{y} \).
Step 3: Substitute values: \( \frac{\sin 50^\circ}{10} = \frac{\sin Y}{8} \).
Step 4: Solve for \(\sin Y\): \( \sin Y = \frac{8 \sin 50^\circ}{10} \approx \frac{8 \times 0.766}{10} \approx 0.6128 \).
Step 5: Find Y by taking the inverse sine: \( Y = \arcsin(0.6128) \approx 37.8^\circ \).
The measure of angle Y is approximately \(38^\circ\).

Example 20 (Area Formula): Using the information in the diagram below, find the measure of the included angle C.

Step 1: Use the area formula: \( \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab \sin C \).
Step 2: Substitute the known values: \( 20 = \frac{1}{2}(10)(8) \sin C \).
Step 3: Simplify: \( 20 = 40 \sin C \).
Step 4: Solve for \(\sin C\): \( \sin C = \frac{20}{40} = \frac{1}{2} \).
Step 5: Find the angle C. The acute angle whose sine is 1/2 is \(30^\circ\). (The obtuse angle \(150^\circ\) is also a possibility, but typically the acute angle is expected unless specified otherwise).
The measure of angle C is \(30^\circ\).

3. Polygons and Quadrilaterals

Polygons are closed figures made of straight line segments. Quadrilaterals are four-sided polygons.

Sum of Interior Angles of an n-sided polygon: \( (n-2) \times 180^\circ \).
Measure of each Interior Angle of a regular n-sided polygon: \( \frac{(n-2) \times 180^\circ}{n} \).
Sum of Exterior Angles of any convex polygon: Always \(360^\circ\).

Example 1: What is the measure of each interior angle of a regular octagon (8 sides)?

Step 1: Use the formula with n=8. \[ \frac{(8-2) \times 180^\circ}{8} = \frac{6 \times 180^\circ}{8} = \frac{1080^\circ}{8} \]
Step 2: Calculate the result: \( 135^\circ \).
Each interior angle of a regular octagon is \(135^\circ\).

Example 2: Find the value of x in the pentagon shown below.

Step 1: Identify the number of sides of the polygon. This is a pentagon, so it has \(n=5\) sides.
Step 2: Use the formula for the sum of the interior angles of a polygon: \( \text{Sum} = (n-2) \times 180^\circ \).
Step 3: Calculate the total sum for a pentagon: \( \text{Sum} = (5-2) \times 180^\circ = 3 \times 180^\circ = 540^\circ \).
Step 4: Add the known angles from the diagram: \( 110^\circ + 120^\circ + 130^\circ + 90^\circ = 450^\circ \).
Step 5: Subtract the sum of the known angles from the total sum to find x: \( x = 540^\circ - 450^\circ = 90^\circ \).
The value of x is \(90^\circ\).

Example 3: Find the value of x in the diagram below, using the properties of exterior angles.

Step 1: Recall that the sum of the exterior angles of any convex polygon is always \(360^\circ\).
Step 2: Add the known exterior angles shown in the diagram: \( 80^\circ + 100^\circ + 90^\circ = 270^\circ \).
Step 3: Set up an equation to find x: \( 270^\circ + x = 360^\circ \).
Step 4: Solve for x: \( x = 360^\circ - 270^\circ = 90^\circ \).
The value of x is \(90^\circ\).

Properties of Quadrilaterals

Parallelogram: Opposite sides parallel and equal. Diagonals bisect each other. Area = base \(\times\) height.
Rectangle: A parallelogram with four right angles. Diagonals are equal.
Square: A rectangle with four equal sides. Diagonals are perpendicular bisectors of each other.
Rhombus: A parallelogram with four equal sides. Diagonals are perpendicular bisectors of the angles.
Trapezoid: Has exactly one pair of parallel sides (bases). Area = \(\frac{1}{2}(b_1+b_2)h\).

Example 4: In the parallelogram ABCD shown below, find the values of x and y.

Step 1: Recall the properties of a parallelogram. Opposite angles are equal, and consecutive angles are supplementary (add up to 180Â°).
Step 2: Opposite angles \(\angle A\) and \(\angle C\) are equal. Set up an equation to solve for x. \[ 5x - 20 = 3x + 40 \]
Step 3: Solve the equation for x.
\[ 2x = 60 \]
\[ x = 30 \]
Step 4: Now find the measure of \(\angle A\). \[ \angle A = 5(30) - 20 = 150 - 20 = 130^\circ \]
Step 5: Consecutive angles \(\angle A\) and \(\angle D\) are supplementary. Set up an equation to solve for y. \[ \angle A + \angle D = 180^\circ \] \[ 130^\circ + y^\circ = 180^\circ \]
Step 6: Solve for y. \[ y = 180 - 130 = 50 \]
The values are \(x = 30\) and \(y = 50\).

Example 5: In parallelogram ABCD, \(\angle A = (2x+10)^\circ\) and \(\angle C = (3x-20)^\circ\). Find \(\angle B\).

Step 1: Opposite angles are equal: \( 2x + 10 = 3x - 20 \implies x = 30 \).
Step 2: Find \(\angle A = 2(30) + 10 = 70^\circ \).
Step 3: Consecutive angles are supplementary: \( \angle A + \angle B = 180^\circ \).
Step 4: \( 70^\circ + \angle B = 180^\circ \implies \angle B = 110^\circ \).
The measure of \(\angle B\) is \(110^\circ\).
Trapezoid Properties: A trapezoid is a quadrilateral with exactly one pair of parallel sides (bases). The height (h) is the perpendicular distance between the bases.
Area Formula: \( \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}(b_1+b_2)h \)

Example 6: A trapezoid has bases of length 8 and 14, and a height of 5. What is its area?

Step 1: Use the area formula for a trapezoid: \( \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}(b_1+b_2)h \).
Step 2: Substitute values: \( \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}(8+14)(5) = \frac{1}{2}(22)(5) \).
Step 3: Calculate: \( \text{Area} = 11 \times 5 = 55 \).
The area of the trapezoid is 55 square units.

Example 7: In the trapezoid ABCD shown below, EF is the median. Find the value of x.

Step 1: Recall the property of a trapezoid's median. The length of the median is the average of the lengths of the two bases: \( \text{Median} = \frac{\text{base}_1 + \text{base}_2}{2} \).
Step 2: Substitute the given expressions from the diagram into the formula: \[ 14 = \frac{(x+2) + (3x-2)}{2} \]
Step 3: Simplify the numerator: \[ 14 = \frac{4x}{2} \] \[ 14 = 2x \]
Step 4: Solve for x: \[ x = \frac{14}{2} = 7 \]
The value of x is 7.

Example 8: A trapezoid has bases of length 10 and 18, and a height of 7. Find its area.

Step 1: Recall the area formula for a trapezoid: \( \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}(b_1 + b_2)h \).
Step 2: Substitute the given values for the bases (\(b_1 = 10, b_2 = 18\)) and the height (\(h=7\)): \[ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}(10 + 18)(7) \]
Step 3: Simplify the expression: \[ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}(28)(7) = 14 \times 7 = 98 \]
The area of the trapezoid is 98 square units.
Rhombus Properties: A rhombus is a parallelogram with four equal sides. Its diagonals are perpendicular bisectors of each other.
Area Formula: \( \text{Area} = \frac{d_1 \times d_2}{2} \)

Example 9: The diagonals of a rhombus are 10 cm and 24 cm. What is the perimeter of the rhombus?

Step 1: The diagonals of a rhombus are perpendicular bisectors of each other. They form four congruent right triangles in the center.
Step 2: The legs of each right triangle are half the length of the diagonals: \(10/2 = 5\) cm and \(24/2 = 12\) cm.
Step 3: The side of the rhombus is the hypotenuse of these right triangles. Use the Pythagorean theorem: \(c^2 = 5^2 + 12^2 = 25 + 144 = 169\).
Step 4: The side length is \(c = \sqrt{169} = 13\) cm.
Step 5: The perimeter is 4 times the side length: \(P = 4 \times 13 = 52\) cm.
The perimeter of the rhombus is 52 cm.

Example 10: Find the area of the rhombus shown in the diagram below.

Step 1: Identify the lengths of the half-diagonals from the diagram. Let the center be O. The lengths are \(OC = 15\) and \(OB = 8\).
Step 2: The diagonals of a rhombus bisect each other. Calculate the full length of each diagonal.
Diagonal 1 (\(d_1\)) = \(2 \times OC = 2 \times 15 = 30\).
Diagonal 2 (\(d_2\)) = \(2 \times OB = 2 \times 8 = 16\).
Step 3: Use the area formula for a rhombus: \( \text{Area} = \frac{d_1 \times d_2}{2} \). \[ \text{Area} = \frac{30 \times 16}{2} = \frac{480}{2} = 240 \]
The area of the rhombus is 240 square units.

4. Coordinate Geometry

Coordinate geometry applies algebraic principles to geometric shapes. It involves using the coordinate plane to find lengths, midpoints, and prove properties of shapes.

Formulas on the Coordinate Plane

For two points \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\):
Distance Formula: \(d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}\)
Midpoint Formula: \(M = \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)\)
Slope Formula: \(m = \frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}\)

Example 1: Find the length of the line segment connecting the points A(1, -2) and B(7, 6).

Step 1: Use the distance formula. \[ d = \sqrt{(7-1)^2 + (6-(-2))^2} = \sqrt{6^2 + 8^2} \]
Step 2: Calculate the result: \( d = \sqrt{36 + 64} = \sqrt{100} = 10 \).
The length of the segment is 10 units.

Example 2: A line segment has endpoints at (-4, 3) and (2, 9). What are the coordinates of its midpoint?

Step 1: Use the midpoint formula. \[ M = \left(\frac{-4+2}{2}, \frac{3+9}{2}\right) = \left(\frac{-2}{2}, \frac{12}{2}\right) \]
Step 2: Simplify the coordinates: \( M = (-1, 6) \).
The midpoint is at (-1, 6).

Example 3: Find the slope of the line passing through the points (-2, 1) and (4, 10).

Step 1: Use the slope formula: \(m = \frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}\).
Step 2: Substitute the coordinates: \(m = \frac{10-1}{4-(-2)} = \frac{9}{6}\).
Step 3: Simplify the fraction: \(m = \frac{3}{2}\).
The slope of the line is \(\frac{3}{2}\).

Example 4: Line A passes through (1, 2) and (3, 6). Line B passes through (0, 5) and (4, 3). Are the lines parallel, perpendicular, or neither?

Step 1: Find the slope of Line A: \(m_A = \frac{6-2}{3-1} = \frac{4}{2} = 2\).
Step 2: Find the slope of Line B: \(m_B = \frac{3-5}{4-0} = \frac{-2}{4} = -\frac{1}{2}\).
Step 3: Compare the slopes. Parallel lines have equal slopes. Perpendicular lines have slopes that are negative reciprocals (their product is -1).
Step 4: Check the product of the slopes: \(m_A \times m_B = 2 \times (-\frac{1}{2}) = -1\).
Since the product of the slopes is -1, the lines are perpendicular.

Equation of a Circle

The standard equation of a circle with center \((h, k)\) and radius \(r\) is derived directly from the distance formula.

Standard Equation of a Circle:
\( (x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2 \)

Example 5: What is the equation of a circle with a center at (3, -5) and a radius of 4?

Step 1: Identify h=3, k=-5, and r=4.
Step 2: Substitute these values into the standard equation. \[ (x-3)^2 + (y-(-5))^2 = 4^2 \]
Step 3: Simplify the equation. \[ (x-3)^2 + (y+5)^2 = 16 \]
The equation of the circle is \( (x-3)^2 + (y+5)^2 = 16 \).

Example 6: Find the center and radius of the circle given by the equation \( x^2 + y^2 - 6x + 8y - 11 = 0 \).

Step 1: Rearrange and group the x and y terms. \[ (x^2 - 6x) + (y^2 + 8y) = 11 \]
Step 2: Complete the square for both x and y.
For x: \((\frac{-6}{2})^2 = 9\). For y: \((\frac{8}{2})^2 = 16\).
Add these values to both sides of the equation. \[ (x^2 - 6x + 9) + (y^2 + 8y + 16) = 11 + 9 + 16 \]
Step 3: Factor the perfect square trinomials. \[ (x-3)^2 + (y+4)^2 = 36 \]
Step 4: Identify the center (h, k) and radius r.
Center: (3, -4)
Radius: \(r^2 = 36 \implies r = 6\)
The center is (3, -4) and the radius is 6.

Example 7: Write the standard equation for the circle shown in the graph below.

Step 1: Identify the coordinates of the center (h, k) from the graph. The center is marked at (-2, 1). So, h = -2 and k = 1.
Step 2: Identify the radius (r) by measuring the distance from the center to any point on the circle. For example, the distance from the center (-2, 1) to the point (1, 1) on the circle is 3 units. So, r = 3.
Step 3: Substitute h, k, and r into the standard circle equation: \( (x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2 \). \[ (x - (-2))^2 + (y - 1)^2 = 3^2 \]
Step 4: Simplify the equation. \[ (x+2)^2 + (y-1)^2 = 9 \]
The equation of the circle is \( (x+2)^2 + (y-1)^2 = 9 \).

5. Circles

Circles are a set of points equidistant from a center point. Problems often involve circumference, area, arcs, sectors, and angles.

Formulas and Definitions

Circle Formulas (r = radius, d = diameter):
Circumference: \(C = 2\pi r\)
Area: \(A = \pi r^2\)
Arc Length: \(L = \frac{\theta}{360^\circ} \times 2\pi r\)
Sector Area: \(A_{sector} = \frac{\theta}{360^\circ} \times \pi r^2\)

Example 1: A circle has a circumference of \(18\pi\). What is its area?

Step 1: Find the radius from the circumference: \( 18\pi = 2\pi r \implies r = 9 \).
Step 2: Use the radius to find the area: \( A = \pi (9)^2 = 81\pi \).
The area of the circle is \(81\pi\).

Example 2: In a circle with radius 6, what is the area of a sector with a central angle of \(60^\circ\)?

Step 1: Use the sector area formula: \( A_{sector} = \frac{60}{360} \times \pi (6)^2 \).
Step 2: Simplify: \( A_{sector} = \frac{1}{6} \times 36\pi = 6\pi \).
The area of the sector is \(6\pi\).

Angles, Arcs, and Tangents

The measure of a central angle equals its arc. An inscribed angle is half its arc. A tangent line touches the circle at exactly one point and is perpendicular to the radius at that point.

Central vs. Inscribed Angle: The measure of a central angle is equal to its intercepted arc. An inscribed angle that intercepts the same arc is exactly half the measure of the central angle. In the diagram, this relationship is shown as \(2\theta\) for the central angle and \(\theta\) for the inscribed angle.

Example 3: In the circle with center O shown below, the measure of the central angle \(\angle AOB\) is \(70^\circ\). What is the measure of the inscribed angle \(\angle ACB\)?

Step 1: The inscribed angle \(\angle ACB\) and the central angle \(\angle AOB\) both intercept the same arc AB.
Step 2: The measure of an inscribed angle is half the measure of the central angle that intercepts the same arc.
Step 3: Calculate the measure of \(\angle ACB\): \[ \angle ACB = \frac{1}{2} \times \angle AOB = \frac{1}{2} \times 70^\circ = 35^\circ \]
The measure of the inscribed angle \(\angle ACB\) is \(35^\circ\).

Example 4: In a circle, an inscribed angle measures \(25^\circ\). What is the measure of its intercepted arc?

Step 1: The measure of an intercepted arc is twice the measure of the inscribed angle that subtends it.
Step 2: Set up the relationship: Arc Measure = \(2 \times \text{Inscribed Angle}\).
Step 3: Substitute the given value: Arc Measure = \(2 \times 25^\circ = 50^\circ\).
The measure of the intercepted arc is \(50^\circ\).

Example 5: In the diagram below, two inscribed angles \(\angle ADB\) and \(\angle ACB\) intercept the same arc AB. If \(\angle ADB = 40^\circ\), what is the measure of \(\angle ACB\)?

Step 1: Identify the key property. Inscribed angles that intercept the same arc are equal in measure.
Step 2: Both \(\angle ADB\) and \(\angle ACB\) intercept arc AB. Therefore, their measures must be equal. \[ \angle ACB = \angle ADB \]
Step 3: Substitute the known value. \[ \angle ACB = 40^\circ \]
The measure of \(\angle ACB\) is \(40^\circ\).
Tangent Lines: A tangent line is a line that touches a circle at exactly one point, called the point of tangency. A key theorem states that the radius drawn to the point of tangency is always perpendicular (\(90^\circ\)) to the tangent line.

Example 6: A line is tangent to a circle with center O at point P. The radius of the circle is 5 cm. Point Q is on the tangent line such that the distance from O to Q is 13 cm. Find the length of the tangent segment PQ.

Step 1: The radius OP is perpendicular to the tangent line at point P. This forms a right triangle \(\triangle OPQ\), with the right angle at P.
Step 2: In \(\triangle OPQ\), the radius OP is one leg (length 5), the segment OQ is the hypotenuse (length 13), and the tangent segment PQ is the other leg.
Step 3: Use the Pythagorean theorem: \(OP^2 + PQ^2 = OQ^2\). \[ 5^2 + PQ^2 = 13^2 \]
Step 4: Solve for PQ.
\[ 25 + PQ^2 = 169 \]
\[ PQ^2 = 169 - 25 = 144 \]
\[ PQ = \sqrt{144} = 12 \]
The length of the tangent segment PQ is 12 cm.
Arc Length and Sectors: An arc is a portion of the circumference of a circle, and a sector is a pie-shaped region bounded by two radii and the intercepted arc. Their sizes are proportional to the central angle \(\theta\). The formulas are \(L = \frac{\theta}{360^\circ} \times 2\pi r\) for arc length and \(A_{sector} = \frac{\theta}{360^\circ} \times \pi r^2\) for sector area.

Example 8: In a circle with a radius of 9 cm, find the area of the sector whose central angle is 40Â°, as shown in the diagram.

Step 1: Recall the formula for the area of a sector: \(A_{sector} = \frac{\theta}{360^\circ} \times \pi r^2\).
Step 2: Substitute the given values: radius \(r=9\) and central angle \(\theta=40^\circ\). \[ A_{sector} = \frac{40}{360} \times \pi (9)^2 \]
Step 3: Simplify the fraction and calculate the area. \[ A_{sector} = \frac{1}{9} \times 81\pi = 9\pi \]
The area of the sector is \(9\pi\) square cm.

Example 9: In a circle with a radius of 12 cm, find the length of the arc intercepted by a central angle of 150Â°, as shown below.

Step 1: Recall the formula for arc length: \(L = \frac{\theta}{360^\circ} \times 2\pi r\).
Step 2: Substitute the given values: radius \(r=12\) and central angle \(\theta=150^\circ\). \[ L = \frac{150}{360} \times 2\pi (12) \]
Step 3: Simplify the fraction and calculate the length. \[ L = \frac{5}{12} \times 24\pi = 5 \times 2\pi = 10\pi \]
The length of the arc is \(10\pi\) cm.

Example 10: An inscribed angle in a circle intercepts an arc of \(84^\circ\). What is the measure of the inscribed angle?

Step 1: The measure of an inscribed angle is half its intercepted arc.
Step 2: \( \frac{1}{2} \times 84^\circ = 42^\circ \).
The measure of the inscribed angle is \(42^\circ\).

Example 11: A triangle is inscribed in a semicircle. What must be true about the triangle?

Step 1: An angle inscribed in a semicircle always intercepts an arc of \(180^\circ\).
Step 2: The measure of this angle is half the arc: \( \frac{1}{2} \times 180^\circ = 90^\circ \).
The triangle must be a right triangle.

Example 12 (Tangent Circles): Three circles with radius 5 are mutually tangent to each other. What is the area of the triangle formed by connecting their centers?

Step 1: When two circles are tangent, the distance between their centers is the sum of their radii.
Step 2: Since all three circles have the same radius (5), the distance between any two centers is \(5+5=10\).
Step 3: The triangle formed by connecting the centers has three equal sides of length 10. It is an equilateral triangle.
Step 4: The area of an equilateral triangle with side 's' is \( \frac{s^2\sqrt{3}}{4} \). \[ \text{Area} = \frac{10^2\sqrt{3}}{4} = \frac{100\sqrt{3}}{4} = 25\sqrt{3} \]
The area of the triangle is \(25\sqrt{3}\).
